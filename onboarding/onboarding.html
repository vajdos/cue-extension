Cue.

Five behaviors science says other people perceive as good listening — measured live on your device, never recorded, never uploaded.

What Cue measures

During your video calls, Cue measures five subtle acoustic patterns from both sides of the conversation:

Response gap (Stivers 2009, PNAS)
Within-turn pause (Liu 2025, JESP)
Energy mirroring (Pentland 2008)
Interruption count (Sacks et al. 1974)
Pace

Both sides of the conversation

Cue measures rhythm and tone — yours and the person you're talking with. Both stay on your device. Cue does not record audio, does not transcribe words, and does not upload anything.

The browser shows a standard tab-capture indicator whenever Cue is measuring tab audio. That's the signal you'll see during every call.

How the privacy claim holds at the byte level

  • No network calls. Verify with Chrome DevTools → Network tab during any session — you'll see zero outbound traffic from the extension.
  • No audio storage. Audio frames enter the AudioWorklet (Chrome's audio thread), get reduced to numerical features (pace in WPM, energy in RMS, etc.), and are discarded.
  • No transcription. Cue measures acoustic features only — it has no speech-to-text capability and never accesses words.
  • No cloud sync. Cue uses chrome.storage.local and IndexedDB. Nothing leaves your device.
  • Source is public. Audit every line at github.com/vajdos/cue-extension.

About per-context defaults

  • For Default, 1-on-1, Sales, and Presentation contexts → Cue measures both sides by default.
  • For Negotiation, Interview, and Hard conversation contexts → Cue measures only YOU by default; you can opt in to both sides per-session.
  • Switch context anytime via the panel's dropdown during a session.

Optional, anonymized telemetry

  • You can opt in (Settings → Contribute to Listening Corpus) to share anonymized session metrics that help validate which signals predict being heard well. We never collect audio, transcripts, or PII through this channel.
